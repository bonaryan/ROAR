Understand the Model

Climate Hazard

a. Climate Change Scenarios

Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) are climate change scenarios of projected socioeconomic global changes up to 2100 as defined in the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report on climate change in 2021. They are used to derive greenhouse gas emissions scenarios with different climate policies. In ROAR Tool, there are three SSP scenarios: SSP1-2.6 (low GHG emissions); SSP2-4.5 (intermediate GHG emissions); and SSP5-8.5 (very high GHG emissions). SSP 5-8.5 is selected as the default climate change scenario, associated with Business As Usual strategy in GHG emission mitigation.

b. Hazard Assessment

Statistical analyses such as extreme value analysis (EVA) are used. The theorem relates to the limiting distributions for the extremes of a very large collection of independent random variables from the same distribution. This procedure is used to estimate the change of characteristic value of a climate variable (Ck). The concept of characteristic value is actually adopted in the Building Code. Due to climate change, this Ck changes dynamically so we can no longer use the static Ck based on the Building Code. Change Factor (CF) is introduced as the effect quantification of the climate change on climate variables.

c. Climate Variable

Climate data is obtained from stastistically downscaled CMIP6 global climate models from the Pacific Climate Impact Consortium (PCIC), University of Victoria. More variables are being included in the tool.

  • T_MAX: Maximum temperature (celsius)
  • T_MIN: Minimum temperature (celsius)
  • PR_SUM: Total precipitation (mm)
  • HDD_SUM: Heating Degree Days - proxy for energy demand for heating,
  • CDD_SUM: Cooling Degree Days - proxy for energy demand for cooling
  • TMAX34_SUM: Total days above 34 degrees celsius
  • TMAX34_STREAK: Longest number of consecutive days above 34 degrees celsius
  • PR20_SUM: Total days with more than 20 mm of precipitation
  • PR20_STREAK: Longest number of consecutive days with more than 20 mm of precipitation

    • Determine Failure Types

    The interaction between climatic actions and building component would lead to failure. This tool classify failures into three types: 1) Degraded Failure - a failure associated with advanced degradation; 2) Catasthropic Failure - a sudden failure from which recovery of the asset is not feasible; and 3) Energy Change - probability of energy consumption change. We use different model for each module of failure, i.e., Stochastic process, Extreme Value analysis, and Monte-Carlo regression model, respectively.

    Determine Failure Effects

    It’s useful to be able to define the failure in terms of the probability of failure. By applying specific key risk indicator it’s much easier to connect with the consequence of failure, either through return period or the changing service life or the probability of demand change.

    Climate hazard ▸ Failure types ▸ Failure effects ▸ Failure modes

    Finally, we look at the failure modes in relation to their effects, manifested as risk description for each component.

    Risk Level

    In principle, we use classical risk assessment as the reference, multiplying probability of failure of a particular asset by its consequence of failure to get the risk level. The criteria for probability of occurence and consequence adopted criteria defined by the Public Infrastructure Engineering Vulnerability Committee (PIEVC).


    What is ROAR?

    The Resiliency Opportunity Assessment + Response (ROAR) helps building owners and managers quickly assess potential risks from climate change and potential opportunities for improved resiliency, to inform decisions on where to set prioritized resiliency efforts. The tool can identify low and no-cost opportunities that can be implemented immediately, as well as retrofit opportunities that can be investigated further through more detailed audits or studies.

    The tool uses different methodologies to assess different types of risks: (1) Stochastic process for stress risk module; (2) Extreme Value analysis for shock risk module; and (3) Monte-Carlo regression model for energy risk module. Those methods are applied to analyze a building component's data and weather, in order to determine how much probability of failure is expected for its respective function and the consequence(s) that arise as a result.

    The performance of the system and component are then aggregated to obtain the total climate value at risk of the building. In addition to telling you whether a system or component's risk level is high, moderate or low, it goes a step further to tell you why that is the case. If a risk is categorized as high, for example, it can tell you it is because the asset is highly sensitive to the extreme weather, poorly located, or critical in terms of function and monetary value while the no climate change scenario acts as the baseline. With this information, a building owner can take the necessary actions, for example, to adjust the operation scheme of the asset, add protective barriers, do maintenance check and retrofit, perform an engineering audit that focuses on specific asset, or transfer the risk with insurance. As such, building owners and managers have a sound basis to strategically invest in effective measures to enhance resilience and maintain property valuations.
